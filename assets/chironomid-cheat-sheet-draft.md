## PAGE 2 — WHY THIS GUIDE EXISTS

If you've fished Colorado stillwaters for a few seasons, you already know: chironomids are 70–90% of what trout eat most of the year. They show up at every depth in every water column from ice-off through fall, and the bite is reliably *there* when you find it.

The problem isn't whether to fish a chironomid. It's *which one, how deep, and when.*

This sheet is the simple version of what I tell people on guided days. Three rules. Eight patterns. One table. A few minutes of reading.

If you do nothing else from this guide, internalize this:

> **Depth beats pattern. Pattern beats color.**
> Find the column the fish are eating in. Then pick the pattern. Then dial color last.

Most anglers get this backward — they pick a "favorite" pattern, fish it at one depth, and blame the fly when nothing happens. Don't do that.

---

## PAGE 3 — THE THREE RULES

### Rule 1 — Depth is the first decision

Get this wrong and the prettiest fly in your box catches nothing. Use an indicator-and-leader system and treat depth like a slider you adjust every 15 minutes until you're getting eats. Start by setting the bottom fly **1–2 feet off the lake floor**. Work *up* the column until you find them.

**The math:** lake depth minus leader length = how high your fly rides off the bottom. A 14-foot indicator setup in 16 feet of water = bottom fly at 14 feet, 2 feet off the deck. That's your starting point.

When in doubt: deeper than you think. Most anglers fish too shallow.

### Rule 2 — Pattern is the second decision

Once depth is right, swap pattern, not depth. Same column, different bug. Two consecutive blanks on a chironomid? Try the next pattern at the same depth before you decide depth is wrong.

### Rule 3 — Color is the last decision

Color matters less than depth and pattern combined. That said: **stained water + low light = black & red.** **Clear water + sun = brown & gold.** White beads earn in low light. Silver in clear, mid-sun conditions.

Don't overthink color. Get the first two right and the box doesn't matter much.

## PAGE 4 — THE 8 PATTERNS (recipes + when they earn)

### 1. Chocolate Gold

Tungsten-loaded, brown body, gold ribbing, small flash.

- **Hook:** 2x heavy scud/nymph, #12–18
- **Bead:** tungsten gold, sized to hook
- **Body:** brown thread or pearl/copper tinsel
- **Rib:** fine gold wire
- **Wing case (optional):** dark UV resin
- **When it earns:** Spring through summer, water temps 48–62°F. Deeper presentations (12–22 ft). The "if you only carry one" chironomid.

### 2. Snow Cone (Frank's Red Hot Ronnie)

Black thread, red wire, white bead. The single most-recognizable Colorado chironomid silhouette.

- **Hook:** 2x heavy, #12–18
- **Bead:** white tungsten or glass
- **Body:** black thread
- **Rib:** red wire, medium
- **When it earns:** Stained water, low light, early/late in the day. Ice-off through May, and again late August into fall. Aggressive trigger pattern.

### 3. Top Secret Ironman

Built for pressured water. Black body, contrasting flash, durable construction.

- **Hook:** 2x heavy, #8–14 (larger profile)
- **Bead:** tungsten black or copper
- **Body:** black thread or dubbing
- **Rib:** copper or pearl flash, light
- **When it earns:** Heavily pressured lakes (Spinney, 11 Mile) mid-summer. When standard patterns get refused after one drift.

### 4. Burnt Wino

Wine body, copper bead, subtle. Earns when bright patterns get inspected and refused.

- **Hook:** 2x heavy, #14–18
- **Bead:** copper tungsten
- **Body:** wine/maroon thread
- **Rib:** copper wire, fine
- **When it earns:** Clear water mid-day, post-front conditions, technical bites. Often the answer when bigger patterns aren't.

### 5. Burnt Hopper

Tan/olive body, gold flash, hopper-adjacent profile that still drifts like a chironomid.

- **Hook:** 2x heavy, #14–16
- **Bead:** gold tungsten
- **Body:** olive or tan thread
- **Rib:** gold wire, medium
- **When it earns:** Late summer through fall (Aug–Oct), warmer water, terrestrial fall.

### 6. Chirono'midge

Smaller-profile midge — exactly what fish ignore when you're throwing too big.

- **Hook:** 2x heavy, #16–20
- **Bead:** small tungsten silver or black
- **Body:** thin thread, your choice of base color
- **Rib:** ultra-fine wire
- **When it earns:** Selective fish, smaller natural hatches, late spring on clear water.

### 7. Top Secret Winged Duck

Translucent wing case profile, mid-summer pattern.

- **Hook:** 2x heavy, #14–18
- **Bead:** tungsten silver or copper
- **Body:** thread with UV resin wing case
- **Rib:** fine pearl tinsel
- **When it earns:** Summer thermocline conditions when fish hold in the 14–20 ft range and want a pupa about to emerge.

### 8. Muskie Buzzer

Larger, more pronounced, designed for chunky fish in chunky water.

- **Hook:** 2x heavy, #10–14
- **Bead:** tungsten black or red
- **Body:** black thread with red wire
- **Rib:** medium red wire
- **When it earns:** Trophy water (Antero historically, Spinney, Delaney for bigger fish). When you want a louder profile.

> **A note on hooks:** every pattern in this guide is tied on **2x heavy wire**. Not for snobbery — because chironomid eats are subtle and trout often nip on the drop. 2x heavy keeps the bend solid through the hook-set and resists straightening on a 24-inch cutbow. Don't cheap out on hooks.

**Don't be a slave to the table.** Water doesn't read tables. Use it as a starting point — first 30 minutes of any trip you're confirming the table, not blindly following it.
